Australian inflation · 2026

Australia is not feeling one inflation rate.

CPI is often reported as one national number. However, that number does not show where the pressure is stronger, or which everyday costs are driving it. This visualisation starts with city differences, then breaks inflation down into basket groups, monthly movements, wages and rent pressure.

Chapter 1 · City differences

A national CPI number hides local pressure.

The first step is to look at geography. If inflation is only read as one national figure, it is easy to miss that some capital cities face stronger price pressure than others.

CHAPTER 2 · BASKET PROFILE

The same CPI headline can come from different baskets.

After comparing cities, the next question is what creates the difference. Breaking CPI into basket groups shows whether pressure is coming from transport, housing, food, recreation or other spending areas.

3. City × basket heatmap

The heatmap compares each city across CPI basket groups. Darker and warmer cells show stronger price growth, helping reveal which spending areas are shaping each city’s inflation profile.

Annotation: The heatmap moves the story from where inflation is higher to what is driving it. Warmer cells highlight basket groups with stronger local pressure.

4. Selected-city basket wheel

This chart focuses on the selected city. It shows which basket groups are doing more of the work behind the headline CPI number.

For the selected city, the pressure is not spread evenly across the basket.

This is why the story does not stop at the city ranking. Two cities can have similar headline CPI, but the reason behind the pressure may be different. For households, that difference matters because transport, rent and food are felt in different ways.

The selected city is used as a case study: first locate it on the map, then look inside its basket to see what is driving the pressure.
Chapter 3 · National comparison

The national basket gives the baseline.

A local pattern is easier to understand when it is compared with the national basket. This section shows whether the selected category is part of a wider Australian trend or a stronger local pressure.

5. CPI basket dot-matrix

The dot-matrix makes larger CPI movements visually heavier. It highlights which basket groups are contributing most to the national inflation story. Transport stands out as the strongest national pressure, while several other categories remain much lighter. This makes the basket profile uneven rather than evenly spread across all spending groups.

6. Monthly shock vs annual heat

Annual CPI and monthly movement tell different stories. A category can look high over the year but be cooling recently, or look moderate overall while suddenly rising in the latest month.

Following: Transport

This comparison helps explain why inflation can feel confusing. People may hear that annual inflation is easing, but still notice a sharp monthly increase in a specific bill or service.

This section shifts the story from “which category is high?” to “what kind of pressure is this category creating?”
Chapter 4 · Drivers over time

Inflation is a moving mix, not a fixed ranking.

A ranking only captures one moment. The time-based charts show whether pressure is building, cooling, or moving between goods and services.

7. Contribution stream

This stream chart shows how the CPI mix changes over time. Some small gaps appear because the contribution data is not available for every group in every month. I kept the gaps rather than smoothing over them, because connecting missing values would make the pattern look more certain than it really is.

Note: Small gaps appear where contribution data is not available for every basket group. These gaps do not represent zero contribution.

8. Goods vs services pulse strip

The pulse strip separates short-term pressure from goods and services. This makes it easier to see whether recent inflation is coming from physical items or from service costs.

Why the time view matters

Households do not experience inflation as one clean yearly number. Monthly movement shows whether pressure is persistent, temporary, or shifting into a different part of the economy.

Chapter 5 · Everyday essentials

Some categories feel sharper because they affect daily life.

Rent, electricity and fuel are regular bills or sudden price changes. They can make inflation feel stronger than the headline CPI number suggests.

9. Essential shock calendar

The calendar shows that essentials do not move smoothly. Repeated red and blue tiles help explain why households can feel pressure through sudden increases as well as regular bills.

10. Fuel volatility spikes

Fuel is separated because it often changes sharply from month to month. These spikes can make inflation feel unstable even when the annual CPI number looks moderate.

Why volatility matters

People notice repeated bills and sudden price changes, not only the annual CPI figure. This is why the story separates steady inflation pressure from categories that move sharply month to month.

Chapter 6 · Household pressure

Can households absorb the pressure?

The final section connects price changes to income and rent pressure. Inflation becomes a cost-of-living problem when wages do not keep up, or when rent takes a large share of household income.

11. Wage–CPI gap dumbbells

This chart compares CPI with wage growth. When CPI rises faster than wages, households lose purchasing power because income has less room to absorb price increases.

Note: Only periods with both wage and CPI data are shown, so missing wage observations are not treated as zero.

12. Rental stress risk gauge

The 30% line is used as a stress threshold. It shows why rent is not just another CPI category: high rent can reduce a household’s ability to absorb other price increases.

Ending insight

Overall, I found that CPI is useful as a headline, but it does not explain how inflation is actually felt. The story becomes clearer only after breaking it down by city, basket group, monthly movement, wages and rent pressure.
